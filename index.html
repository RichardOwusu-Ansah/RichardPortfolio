About Me

I am Richard Owusu-Ansah, a Geographer and Geospatial Data Scientist pursuing a Master of Science in Geography and Environment at The George Washington University. My research focuses on environmental predictive modeling, with a specialization in urban flood risk assessments, urban heat islands, carbon dynamics, and land use/land cover analysis. Proficient in Python, ArcGIS, QGIS, machine learning, and statistical analysis, I have contributed to projects on land cover change, flooding, illegal mining, and urban heat islands. My work leverages spatial, statistical, and climate models to explore the interactions between climate change and urban environments, aiming to enhance urban resilience to environmental stressors. In addition to research, I am passionate about teaching. As a Graduate Teaching Assistant at The George Washington University, I facilitate Physical Geography lab sessions, guiding students through hands-on learning and practical exercises to deepen their understanding of key geographical concepts.

I love everything GIS, and I am currently exploring GeoAI and how I can integrate it into my work.

Research Experience

Masters Thesis: Advancing Flood Risk Prediction through Geospatial Modeling: A Case Study of Greater Boston Area

The George Washington University

August 2024 - Present

  • Objective: Develop a comprehensive geospatial flood model to assess vulnerable areas and quantify potential economic losses in high flood-prone areas outside of FEMA-designated flood zones.
  • Utilized ArcGIS Pro and Python’s Arcpy module to conduct spatial analyses and automate workflows.
  • Leveraged DEM to conduct slope analysis and hydrological modeling to map flood-prone areas based on elevation, soil type, land use, storm surge, precipitation, climate projections, and distance to streams.
  • Employed confusion matrix and multi-metric performance analysis to compare high-risk flood zones in my model with FEMA-designated flood zones to identify spatial agreement and discrepancies.
  • Integrated social vulnerability data to estimate vulnerable populations within high flood-risk zones outside of National Flood Hazard Layer zones.
  • Utilized property value data to estimate the potential economic losses in high flood-risk zones overlooked by FEMA.

Mapping Urban Food Deserts in Washington, D.C.: A Multi-Criteria Evaluation Approach to Assessing Food Access and Vulnerability

Food deserts have emerged as a significant focus of concern for public health as well as urban planning in the past two decades. Studies have indicated that food deserts are more common in low-income communities and communities of color within urban environments. In this paper, we explore the food desert issue in Washington, DC, the nation’s capital. We utilized a multi-criteria evaluation approach and GIS to map and identify areas in the metropolitan area that are food deserts.

RiverSmart Homes: A Green Infrastructure Approach to Urban Water Management, The Case of the Anacostia River in Washington DC

This research project explores the role of green infrastructure in addressing urban water management challenges. Focusing on the Anacostia River, the study examines how the RiverSmart Homes program utilizes Low Impact Development (LID) designs—such as rain barrels, rain gardens, and permeable pavers—to mitigate stormwater runoff and improve water quality. The research highlights the program's success in reducing pollution, enhancing stormwater capture, and promoting water reuse, while also addressing challenges related to equity and community engagement. The findings underscore the importance of green infrastructure in fostering sustainable urban environments and restoring ecologically degraded waterways.

National Park Accessibility Analysis

Utilized Python to conduct an analysis of national park accessibility within 50 miles across all states in the US. The project analyzed the percentage of each state's population living within 50 miles of a national park. Additionally, it examined the racial aspect of park accessibility by calculating the percentage of white and Black populations within 50 miles of parks. The results include detailed tables and charts to visualize the findings.
